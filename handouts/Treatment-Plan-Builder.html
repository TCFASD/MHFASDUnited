Step 1

Start here: what's bringing them in

The first question is simple: what's the problem? But a person's struggles are rarely theirs alone, so we ask it for everyone who is in it. These answers point to what to look at next.

Who is this plan for?

This decides whether caregiver questions and the caregiver column show up. You can change it later.

What's going on for the client?

Tap any that fit to use them as a starting point, then add your own. These surface the brain rows that often sit underneath, on Step 3.

What's going on for the caregiver and family?

The strain rarely stays with one person. Worn out, walking on eggshells, constant conflict, feeling like they are failing, the toll on siblings or the relationship.

What the tool does with this. Instead of putting the whole brain checklist in front of you, Step 3 highlights the rows that tend to sit underneath what you just described. The presenting problem points to where to look. You still make the call.
Step 2

Who's at the table

Before any goals, decide who the plan is really for. Often, especially with kids and with anyone who leans on a support system, a caregiver or another adult has to be in it. Not as a bystander, but with their own part to play. Roles can shift over time, so note when.

A caregiver might be very hands-on early while things get set up, then step back as the person grows. Or the other way around. The "when" is part of the plan.
Step 3

Take stock of the brain

No lingo needed. These are plain descriptions of what you actually see. Check what sounds like your client. Highlighted rows are the ones your Step 1 answers suggested looking at first, but check anything that fits. Fill the caregiver column too, honestly. We need a real read on what's workable, not a diagnosis.

Why there's a caregiver column. We routinely ask caregivers to do things their own brain, or their bandwidth on a hard week, can't reliably pull off. Stay calm while your child is melting down. Keep the routine identical seven days a week. Remember the new strategy in the moment. If the plan leans on something the caregiver can't actually do, the plan isn't realistic. Better to know up front than to set them up to fail.
What it can look like, day to day Client Caregiver / adult

For the caregiver, a few of these read even plainer: "Hard to stay calm when my child is melting down." "Hard to keep things the same every day." "I get overloaded too."

Step 4

Is the behavior a signal?

This is where it's tempting to jump straight to "reduce the behavior." Slow it down. For each behavior that brought them in, ask the same plain questions. Is this just how the brain works right now, or is it a stress reaction to an ask that doesn't fit?

The whole idea, taught through the questions instead of the vocabulary: before we write a goal to make a behavior go away, we look at why it's there. Could it be telling us the expectations don't fit?
Step 5

The referral goal, and the goals that do the work

Schools and insurers need a measurable goal, usually about the behavior that got the person referred. Keep it. We just don't stop there. Underneath it we build the goals that actually change the fit.

The referral goal (keep it measurable)

Example: "Reduce outbursts at school by 50% this semester." Fine. Keep it.

Working from a treatment you already know?

Many approaches come with a standard goal baked in. Check that goal against this brain before you use it. The tool shows the common goal; you decide if it fits, resizes, or gets paired with driver goals.

Driver goals: the ones that change the fit

What happens right before the behavior? A transition, a five-step worksheet, the cafeteria, being told to "use your words" when words are the hard part? Build goals for the environment, the adult's response, and something small for the client. Each names who holds it.

Say it plainly in the plan: we expect the behavior to drop because the fit got better, not because we drilled the person harder. The measurable goal stays. The work underneath it is what changes.
Step 6

Does this fit both brains?

Last pass before it's done. For every goal, two questions. Does it ask the client's brain to do something it can't yet do? And does it lean on the caregiver doing something beyond what they can realistically pull off? A plan only counts as realistic when it fits both. And we keep the client's voice in it, even when a caregiver is central.

If "be consistent seven days a week" isn't real for this caregiver, the plan needs a different kind of support, not a caregiver set up to fail.
