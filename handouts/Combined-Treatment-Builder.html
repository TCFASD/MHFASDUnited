Step 2

PAE / FASD screening

Screening script and questions, matched to who's involved.

▼

Full source material lives in the pae-fasd-screening skill.

Why we ask: prenatal alcohol exposure is one of the most common, and most commonly missed, contributors to the neurodevelopmental profile this tool helps you think through. Naming it early, even when the answer is "unknown," changes how we understand behavior, tailor treatment, and connect the family to the right supports. This isn't about assigning blame. Ask the same way in every case, not just ones that "look at risk." That's what keeps it from feeling like an accusation.

Auto-set from Step 1's caregiver question, but select any that apply if you need more than one script (e.g. a caregiver and an involved birth mother).

Screening through the adoptive/kinship caregiver

Opener (adapt either, before moving into the questions alongside other routine birth-history items):

  1. "As part of getting to know your child's story, I ask a few questions about the pregnancy, birth, and early experiences after birth. These questions help me understand your child's early environment and how I can best support them. You're welcome to answer as much or as little as feels comfortable."

Short screening questions:

  1. "How much alcohol did [child]'s mother consume before she knew she was pregnant?"
  2. "How much alcohol did [child]'s mother consume after she knew she was pregnant?"
  3. "How do you know? (Did you witness use? Is there indirect evidence such as a DUI, records, or a reliable informant?)"

Direct screening with the birth mother

Opener:

  1. "I'll ask a few questions about your pregnancy and your child's early life after birth. Understanding these early experiences can help me better support your child. Please take your time, and feel free to share only what you feel comfortable with."

Short screening questions:

  1. "How far along were you before you found out you were pregnant?"
  2. "Before you knew you were pregnant, how much alcohol did you drink?"
  3. "After you found out you were pregnant, how much alcohol did you drink?"
  4. Any disclosure = a positive screen. No need to quantify to act on it.

If it's positive, one way to respond:

  1. "Thank you for sharing that with me. I know that's not always easy to talk about. Many families have had similar experiences. It doesn't mean anything is wrong, but it can be helpful for me to keep it in mind as I support your child's development."

Screening with the birth father (not currently with the birth mother)

Draft wording, not yet reviewed. A birth father can't answer direct pregnancy questions the way a birth mother can, so this is adapted from the indirect/observational approach (what did you witness or learn) rather than the birth-mother script's first-person pregnancy questions. Flagged for wording review before this is considered settled.

Opener:

  1. "I'd like to ask a few questions about [child]'s early history, based on what you knew or were around for during the pregnancy."

Short screening questions:

  1. "Were you in contact with [child]'s birth mother during the pregnancy?"
  2. "What do you know about alcohol use during the pregnancy, if anything?"
  3. "How do you know that? Did you witness it directly, or is it something she or someone else told you?"

If it's positive, one way to respond:

  1. "Thank you for telling me. I know that's not always easy to talk about, especially secondhand. It doesn't mean anything is wrong, but it can be helpful for me to keep it in mind as I support [child]'s development."

No consistent caregiver, talking to a case manager or case worker

Opener, since the case manager wasn't present for the pregnancy and is being asked to help piece together a record, not to disclose personal history:

  1. "I'd like to ask a few questions about [child]'s background before they came into care. Some of this might be in the file, and some you might not know, and that's fine too. Anything you can tell me helps me take better care of [child]."

Questions and steps, noted in the Home Visitors guide as "most common with foster and adoptive families":

  1. "Is there anything in the case file about the birth mother's alcohol use during the pregnancy?"
  2. "When did the birth mother find out she was pregnant, if that's documented?"
  3. "Do you know anything about the birth mother's history with alcohol, before or after this pregnancy?"
  4. Ask the family or agency to review child welfare documents, birth records, and legal records. Exposure is sometimes documented but gets overlooked.
  5. Check for growth issues (short stature, low weight), intellectual disability, seizure disorder, or microcephaly.
  6. Document the outcome regardless of what you find. Use one of four labels: Confirmed, Suspected, Unconfirmed with red flags, or No information.

Adult self-report, no reliable prenatal history

Real BSI-FASD items still not sourced. Citation for contacting the authors: Widder et al. (2021), Evaluation of the German biographic screening interview for FASD (BSI-FASD), Scientific Reports 11:5233, doi.org/10.1038/s41598-021-83942-2. Corresponding author Michael Widder, Dept. of Psychiatry & Psychotherapy, FAU Erlangen-Nürnberg, Germany. Until those items are in hand, questions below are a working draft, not verbatim BSI-FASD.

Opener:

  1. "I'm going to ask a few questions about your own early life and your family. Some of these you might not know the answer to, and that's fine. Just tell me what you do know."

Questions below are written in plain, simple language on purpose, since processing complex or multi-part questions is often one of the very things this screen is trying to identify. Direct history, asked in third person (the adult is reporting on their mother's pregnancy secondhand):

  1. "Do you know anything about when your mom was pregnant with you?"
  2. "Was your mom trying to have a baby, or did it happen as a surprise?"
  3. "Was your mom going through a hard time when she was pregnant with you? Like a divorce, a big fight, or something scary?"
  4. "Is your mom still alive? Is your dad still alive?"
  5. "Do you know how many weeks or months your mom was pregnant before she found out?"
  6. "Do you know if your mom drank alcohol before she found out she was pregnant? Did she drink after she found out?"
  7. "Did your mom or dad drink alcohol when you were growing up? Did you ever see them get really drunk?"

Adapted from the Home Visitors guide's supplemental questions:

  1. "Do you know if your mom used drugs when she was pregnant with you?"
  2. "Were you a small baby? Did you have trouble growing when you were little?"
  3. "Were you adopted, or did you live in foster care as a kid?"
  4. "Does anyone in your family have brain, learning, or mental health problems?"

Original, inspired by the BSI-FASD's domain structure, not verbatim:

  1. "Did you learn to walk or talk later than other kids?"
  2. "Has a doctor ever told you that you have ADHD, a learning problem, or a mental health problem?"
  3. "Is it hard for you to keep track of money, remember your schedule, or finish plans, even when you're trying hard?"
  4. "Have you had trouble keeping a job or a place to live, even when you're trying hard?"

Brain function

Brain-function ratings for the client, and the caregiver if present.

▼
Before rating anything, take a moment to look at what already exists. Ask the individual or family whether they have any past evaluations, an IEP, or a neuropsych, OT, speech-language, or school evaluation on hand. If testing exists, use it to inform the ratings below. If it doesn't, you can still fill this out from direct observation.
Assessment.html: common assessments and what they measure, who administers each, and where results are usually found
Why the caregiver gets a different set of questions The client's brain-function profile below shapes what the plan can realistically ask of them. For the caregiver, this tool asks a different set of questions instead of running the same domain ratings: how they take in information best, how much time they need to process something new, and how comfortable they are with the pace of a session. That's what actually shapes whether a plan can be delivered consistently at home, and it keeps the caregiver's own experience out of clinical-sounding severity language.

Steps 5–6

Treatment modality, fit, and roles

Pick a modality, compare its brain demands to the profile, set adapted goals and roles.

▼

One causal chain, in this order: the modality's brain demands are compared against the Step 4 profile, that comparison decides the roles (who covers what), and only then do the adapted goals get written, so the goal wording can reflect the role split instead of guessing at it first.

Research and practiceA few of these modalities have research done specifically with FASD. Most don't yet, because that kind of research takes years to build. This step is about thinking through what fits this person's brain, using real clinical experience alongside whatever research exists.
