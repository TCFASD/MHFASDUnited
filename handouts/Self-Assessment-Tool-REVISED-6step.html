Brain-Based Practice Self-Assessment

A snapshot of where you are on the journey

This is a snapshot to orient you, not a test. It mirrors the six steps of the brain-based journey, so your answers point you to where to focus next. There are no right or wrong answers. Rate yourself on what you actually do, not what you know or intend.

Nothing is sent anywhere. If you save your snapshot, it stays in this browser on this device, so when you come back you can see how things have shifted.

1 = Not yet 2 = Emerging 3 = Developing 4 = Integrated
Step 1

Understanding FASD & where you are

Why brain-based practice matters, and your own starting point.
I can explain how prenatal alcohol or drug exposure, trauma, and brain development affect the clients and families I serve.
I can tell the difference, in my own practice, between being brain-based informed and brain-based responsive.
I assume FASD may be present and undiagnosed in my caseload, and I stay alert to it.
Step 2

Preparing for therapy

Setting up the relationship before the work begins.
My consent process is accessible to someone who processes language differently: plain language, checked for understanding.
I prepare the person for therapy, not just the paperwork, before the work formally begins.
Step 3

Assessment

An ongoing, brain-based process that includes the whole system.
I screen for prenatal alcohol exposure and other brain-based factors, and I revisit assessment as the work continues.
When I work with a child or a dependent adult, I treat the family and support system as part of the assessment.
I recognize when executive functioning, sensory processing, or regulation differences may be driving behavior.
Step 4

Treatment planning

Plans matched to capacity that flex as you learn.
I set goals to the client's current developmental capacity, not their chronological age or assumed potential.
I revisit and adjust the treatment plan as I learn what this brain can actually do.
I write caregiver and environmental goals, and I keep them realistic for a hard week, not a perfect one.
Step 5

Treatment

Adapting the work to the brain, and supporting the client's voice.
I adapt evidence-based modalities (CBT, DBT, PCIT, and others) to the brain in front of me rather than expecting the client to adapt to the method.
When a session stalls, I adjust my approach or objective instead of asking the client to try harder.
I actively support the client's own voice and self-advocacy.
Step 6

Creating a reflective practice

Practice, community, and sustaining growth.
I regularly check whether my expectations are realistic given brain-based differences ("can't" vs "won't").
I seek consultation or community when I feel stuck, frustrated, or judgmental.
I use reflection to actually change my practice over time, not just to process feelings.
