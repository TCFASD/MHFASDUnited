For Mental Health Clinicians

You may be treating someone whose brain was shaped by prenatal exposure and never know it. Brain-based responsive care meets the brain in front of you, diagnosed or not.

When Nothing Works

You are a competent clinician with solid training and evidence-based interventions. But your go-to approaches are not landing with certain clients, and neurobiology might be why.

Most therapeutic models were built around typical brain functioning. They have real limits when the brain works differently. That might look like:

  • CBT falling flat because cause-and-effect reasoning is impaired
  • DBT skills that do not generalize due to executive functioning limitations
  • Trauma processing that stalls
  • Behavioral plans that backfire when consequences feel arbitrary

The issue is not your competence. The issue is that your training likely did not teach you to assess and understand the brain before choosing how to treat. Don't let your best approach be your last resort.

Brain-Based Practice: From Understanding to Action

A guided learning journey that takes you from understanding why brain-based practice matters to confidently adapting your clinical work. Each step builds on the last. Working with the resources in each step is the learning, and a short reflection helps it stick.

You do not need to complete every step before helping clients. Even the first step will shift how you see and respond to complexity.

An ongoing process, not a one-time event

Treatment Planning

Plans that flex as you learn

A brain-based plan starts with an honest look at what the client's brain can actually do right now, then sets goals to match. Because you keep assessing, the plan keeps flexing. The aim is a plan that is measurable and medically defensible and matched to the brain in front of you.

Frequently Asked Questions

I do not work with FASD specifically. Is this still relevant?
Yes. The prevalence of FASD is far higher than most clinicians realize, and the vast majority of affected individuals have never been diagnosed. There is a strong chance you are already working with someone whose brain was shaped by prenatal alcohol exposure without either of you knowing it. Beyond FASD, the brain-based framework applies to any client whose brain works differently, including autism, ADHD, intellectual disabilities, learning differences, traumatic brain injury, and complex trauma. The clinical skills you build here transfer broadly, because the core question is always the same: how does this person's brain actually work, and how do I adapt my approach to meet it?
How does this relate to trauma-informed care?
Brain-based practice builds on and extends trauma-informed care. Trauma-informed care changed the question from "what is wrong with you" to "what happened to you." Brain-based practice adds "how does your brain process what happened, and what does it need now?" The two frameworks are complementary.
Will this work with my therapeutic approach?
This does not replace your existing modalities. It helps you adapt what you already use, whether that is CBT, DBT, EMDR, play therapy, or another approach, so it fits the neurobiological reality of the client in front of you.
Do I need to go through every step in order?
Not necessarily. The self-assessment helps you identify where you already are. If you have strong foundational knowledge but struggle with treatment planning, you can focus there. The steps build on each other, but your development will not be uniform across all of them, and that is normal.
